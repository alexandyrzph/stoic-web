The Art of Living

Stoicism teaches us to focus on what we can control and accept what we cannot. It's not about suppressing emotions, but about understanding them and responding with wisdom. A philosophy born in ancient Athens, refined in Rome, and relevant in every age.

01

Marcus Aurelius

121 - 180 AD

Emperor & Philosopher

"You have power over your mind—not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength."

The last of the Five Good Emperors of Rome, Marcus Aurelius ruled during one of the most turbulent periods in Roman history. Despite facing wars, plague, and political intrigue, he maintained his commitment to Stoic principles. His personal writings, known as the Meditations, were never intended for publication but have become one of the most influential works of philosophy.

  • Wrote "Meditations" during military campaigns
  • Ruled Rome during the Antonine Plague
  • Maintained philosophical integrity while wielding absolute power
  • Embodied the concept of the "philosopher king"

02

Epictetus

50 - 135 AD

Slave Turned Teacher

"It's not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters."

Born into slavery in Hierapolis (modern-day Turkey), Epictetus became one of the most influential Stoic philosophers. After gaining his freedom, he established his own school in Nicopolis where he taught that philosophy is not just theory but a way of life. His teachings were recorded by his student Arrian in two works: the Discourses and the Enchiridion (Handbook).

  • Rose from slavery to become a renowned philosopher
  • Emphasized practical ethics over abstract theory
  • Taught the dichotomy of control—what's in our power vs. what's not
  • Influenced Marcus Aurelius and countless others

03

Seneca

4 BC - 65 AD

Statesman & Writer

"We suffer more often in imagination than in reality."

Lucius Annaeus Seneca was a Roman Stoic philosopher, statesman, and dramatist who served as advisor to Emperor Nero. Born in Cordoba, Spain, he became one of Rome's wealthiest and most powerful men. His extensive writings on Stoicism—including essays, letters, and plays—made philosophy accessible to a wider audience. Despite his wealth and status, he faced exile, political intrigue, and ultimately was forced to take his own life.

  • Wrote numerous essays on practical Stoic ethics
  • Authored 124 "Moral Letters to Lucilius"
  • Served as advisor to Emperor Nero
  • Explored themes of time, death, and how to live well

THE JOURNEY

300 BC

Birth in Athens

Zeno of Citium founded Stoicism in Athens, teaching from the Stoa Poikile (Painted Porch), from which the philosophy derives its name.

155 BC

Arrives in Rome

Stoicism spreads to Rome, where it finds fertile ground among politicians, soldiers, and citizens seeking practical wisdom.

50 AD

Golden Age

The height of Roman Stoicism with Epictetus teaching freed slaves and citizens, while Seneca advises Emperor Nero.

170 AD

Philosopher King

Marcus Aurelius writes his Meditations while ruling the Roman Empire and defending its borders, proving Stoicism's practical power.

2025 AD

Modern Renaissance

Stoicism experiences a revival as people seek timeless wisdom to navigate modern challenges, stress, and uncertainty.

FOUR VIRTUES

Wisdom

The knowledge of what is good, what is bad, and what is neither. It guides us to make sound decisions and see the world clearly.

Courage

The strength to face difficulty, danger, and uncertainty with grace and determination. It means doing what is right even when it's hard.

Justice

Treating others fairly and working towards the greater good of humanity. We are all part of something larger than ourselves.

Temperance

Practicing self-control and moderation in all things, maintaining balance and avoiding excess in our desires and actions.

DAILY PRACTICES

01

Morning Meditation

Begin each day by contemplating what lies ahead. Prepare your mind for challenges, remind yourself of your principles, and set your intentions with clarity.

"When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: The people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous and surly."

02

Negative Visualization

Imagine losing what you have to appreciate it more fully. This practice builds resilience and gratitude, reminding us that all things are temporary.

Consider what you would do if you lost your health, your loved ones, or your possessions. This makes you treasure them more.

03

Evening Reflection

Review your day before sleep. What did you do well? Where did you fall short? What can you improve tomorrow? Self-examination leads to growth.

"What bad habit have you cured today? What vice have you checked? In what respect are you better?"
